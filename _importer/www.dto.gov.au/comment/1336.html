Talking Digital ID

Share to:

22/07/2015 - 1:04pm
Author: 
DTO Communications

Wouldn’t it be great if you could securely access public services without needing to bring along a dozen forms of ID every time you did? 

We want to deliver a way for citizens and businesses to transact with public services simply and securely.

The solution isn’t simple, but we’re committed to developing a Trusted Digital Identity Framework. And we want the Australian community to help us get there.

To start, we’re convening a roundtable – featuring leaders from the consumer, IT, identity policy, security, government, and business sectors.

Next, we’re organising an ‘identity policy workshop’ to start forging the Framework. 

More details coming soon.

Keep in mind in your discussions that a digital ID doesn't need to be issued or authorised by the Australian government. There's no reason that people couldn't use, for example, a bank ID, credit card ID, driver's license (from state govs), Frequent Flier card, or some other form of ID as their universal digital ID.

Websites are not secured through government-issued credentials - SSL certificates are issued by private organisations - and I've not seen evidence that government is better or more efficient at identity management than organisations with an explicit interest in correctly identifying individuals due to both profit and legislative reasons.

Given the history of government ID cards in Australia, with the Australia card, it'd be far easier (politically and technically), faster and potentially cheaper to integrate with existing ID services than build another one.

While doing this, Australia also needs a serious review of our privacy framework to enforce reporting and consequences for mass privacy breaches. Currently if my ID is compromised via a US (government or corporate) service I get full information fairly quickly (not as quick as should be), but if my ID is compromised here in Australia, there's no requirement for the company or agency to actually tell me. That's a critical issue.

Cheers,

Craig

Andreas Boehmer replied on Thu, 2015-07-30 12:18 Permalink

Comment: 

Alongside looking at solutions for Digital ID it is important to first look at Digital Proof of Identity (DPOI) in general.

Especially for the situations mentioned by John Gardner where citizens conduct occasional business with government, creating and maintaining a dedicated Digital ID is a considerable overhead, while there are possibly easier ways to proof who you are online in other ways, using existing forms of (digital or non-digital) ID. DPOI also offers the element of choice: if citizens are able to pick the way they want to identify themselves in a transaction they will be more likely to do so than if they are locked down to a single Digital ID.

Our team at BDM Victoria has been doing considerable research in this area and for the moment simple DPOI looks like a far more attractive option than establishing a Digital Identity, while offering a high level of assurance.

Does the name "Australia Card" ring any bells ?

I think the answer is a combination of the comments provided above.
The first step is to establish a 100 point eID. The process of manually identifying yourself to a trusted party already exists, passport, driver licence, Credit card etc.

The trusted party needs to be able to administer the new eID, providing certificate based authentication responses to appropriate queries. This could be a bank , credit card provider, Roads and Traffic Authority. Could even be a private entity, provided they can securely identify the entity (note it may not be just a person) and manage the authentication query correctly.

The second part is establishing trust relationships between the government and those trusted entities to establish and eID hierarchy. Fed. Gov trusts State Gov, state Gov trusts local gov, they all trust the big 4 banks , and Australia Post. But no one trusts Facebook and twitter.

Now I can use a login and eID of my choice , and one that I will remember, and can do my Tax Return, Register my car, do my banking and update my address details when I move with a single login.

Simple, Effective , Flexible.

Several people have already voiced their concern about the practicality of achieving a single identity protocol; my concern is that discussions need to occur within organisations, like local governments, about how they will identify people for which purposes. Continuing ad hoc approaches only perpetuate and exacerbate the problems and create substantial backlogs of work required to undo the multiplicity of existing methods.

Asking for the sort of proof of ID that banks require is appropriate for some interactions, over kill for others. But a unified approach combined with information classification needs to be established, even if it's hard going. In my opinion agreeing on a unified approach and implementing it is the single most important factor in digital service success. Without it we are simply using digital for communication about an analogue service stream.
