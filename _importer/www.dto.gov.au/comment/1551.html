Talking Digital ID

Share to:

22/07/2015 - 1:04pm
Author: 
DTO Communications

Wouldn’t it be great if you could securely access public services without needing to bring along a dozen forms of ID every time you did? 

We want to deliver a way for citizens and businesses to transact with public services simply and securely.

The solution isn’t simple, but we’re committed to developing a Trusted Digital Identity Framework. And we want the Australian community to help us get there.

To start, we’re convening a roundtable – featuring leaders from the consumer, IT, identity policy, security, government, and business sectors.

Next, we’re organising an ‘identity policy workshop’ to start forging the Framework. 

More details coming soon.

Brian O'Gallagher replied on Tue, 2015-08-11 10:33 Permalink

Comment: 

There seems to be confusion between identity, trust and systems / information access.

Firstly a national Identity number per citizen has already been established, It's called your Medicare Number. Every person in Australia has a unique Identification Number.

A number of the trust relationships have already been established. The relevant state authorities that administer drivers licences are already trusted identity providers. Australia Post is also a trusted identity provider reviewing and confirming identity information for a number of clients. The Tax Office trusts the banks as it accesses transaction data in real time.

Identity Management does not mean I now access one system that has all my information contained within. It just says that If the system receives a SAML or OAuth request from a trusted party it lets you in based on that parties ability to identify you.

All the parts required to deliver a eID system already exist. All that is required is trust hierarchy model to establish the layers of trusted entities and how they interact.

A little out of date now , but a model that could be followed would be STORK. The concept of European countries and the EU, aligns well with the Federal and State Governments in Australia

See link to : https://www.eid-stork.eu/

David Birch replied on Thu, 2015-08-13 17:54 Permalink

Comment: 

I think it might be helpfu t distinguish between the need for identity in service delivery and the need to demonstrate entitlement to do something. I've written about this in the UK context before:

http://www.chyp.com/special-feature-electronic-voting-electronic-identit...

Some form of "National Entitlement Scheme" that uses sector-specific and cryptographically unlinkable "meaningless but unique numbers" (MBUNs) that bind to attributes (eg, this person is entitled to work here or this person is over 18 and so on) might provide levels of security and privacy that cannot be simultaneously delivered using the kind of national identity scheme rejected by Australians in the past.

Given that we now live in a world of biometric authentication, smartphones and always-on internet, there is no reason to start from the same place as before. Australia can build on experiences from the UK (IDA), US (NSTIC) and elsewhere to start planning for a next-generation service that is more advanced as it has done with the Document Verification Service (DVS) with its support for attribute assertion and with the immediate payment service.

Drew Andison replied on Wed, 2015-08-19 09:58 Permalink

Comment: 

If you wanted to build a workable federated digital identity system in Australia, international experience (other than in those countries where they have a legislated national eID) suggests that you need some sort of trust framework.

If you wanted to design a trust framework you would start with a set of principles about how such a framework would operate.

At our round table the other week we provided participants with the following principles for their consideration.

The round table involved senior representatives from the consumer, financial, telecommunications, privacy, state government and security sectors.

We are really keen to hear what people think of these principles so we will not only be following what you have to say on this blog but exposing the principles through other online discussion forums.

The principles have been developed to support a secure, simple-to-use online environment where users, government and businesses trust each other, based on common standards, policies, technologies and processes to obtain and use digital identities.

User Centric:

  • Accessing government services must be easy, convenient and simple.

  • Users can choose their credential service providers from a range of accredited government and private sector providers.

  • Users can choose to maintain multiple identifiers and credentials.

  • Personal and business dealings can be combined or kept separate.

Voluntary and Transparent:

  • Users choose whether or not to participate.

  • Users control their online identities.

  • Credential use records are accessible to users.

Privacy Enhancing:

  • Privacy enhancing technology, policy and processes are applied.

  • Personal information must be collected and used only with informed consent, using only the minimum amount of information necessary

  • Users have an informed understanding of how their personal information will be used and protected

Collaborative:

  • Active collaboration between the public and private sectors and the broader community will draw on the respective strengths and expertise of government and business and reflect the strengths and other characteristics of Australia’s current identity system.

Make Best Use of Technology:

  • Adopt emerging technologies and draw on a range of biographical, biometric and other ‘social footprint’ data to deliver flexible, efficient and scalable digital identity solutions.

  • The online identity system is architected to support secure transactions across the whole eco-system, including transactions ranging from anonymous to fully authenticated and from low to high value.

Interoperable:

  • Develop robust policies and technologies which facilitate interconnectedness with other products and systems nationally and internationally.

Future Proofing:

  • The digital identity framework must be adaptable to ensure it can evolve in line with changing technology trends.

Service Delivery:

  • Highly reputable credential providers provide choice and convenience for users.

  • Participation is cost effective for public and private sectors.

Secure and Resilient:

  • Apply minimum standards and accreditation and certification processes to all participants.

  • Relevant existing standards and frameworks will be leveraged wherever appropriate.

  • Sophisticated technologies ensure integrity of the system.

Competitive Marketplace:

  • Highly reputable credential providers will compete to provide choice and convenience for users.
