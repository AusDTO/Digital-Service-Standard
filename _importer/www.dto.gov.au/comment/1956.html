Talking Digital ID

Share to:

22/07/2015 - 1:04pm
Author: 
DTO Communications

Wouldn’t it be great if you could securely access public services without needing to bring along a dozen forms of ID every time you did? 

We want to deliver a way for citizens and businesses to transact with public services simply and securely.

The solution isn’t simple, but we’re committed to developing a Trusted Digital Identity Framework. And we want the Australian community to help us get there.

To start, we’re convening a roundtable – featuring leaders from the consumer, IT, identity policy, security, government, and business sectors.

Next, we’re organising an ‘identity policy workshop’ to start forging the Framework. 

More details coming soon.

That's nice Drew,

As someone who tries to stay across these ID things in 10 languages and cultures, it's always useful to have some guiding principles up front. Saves so many words. So these are ones the DTO is starting from. https://www.gov.uk/design-principles

And we already have DTO's "primary" design principles https://www.dto.gov.au/standard/design-principles

The "ID" design is one side of the "trust framework" design. "Shared Services" are the flip side.
Both must share the same guiding principles.

You talk about "countries where they have a legislated national eID" ("Syndicated ID" to use the entrenched terminology) There they talk about "citizens", unlike UK, US and Aus who talk about "Federated ID" and "Users".

Eventually, gov must inter-operate with other govs.
So a compromise principle for ID would be "Federated not Syndicated." (explains that the two are quite different, yet must eventually inter-operate) You could take the euro approach and consider "The principle of Mutual Recognition" if you're looking to the bigger picture. (Clause 14 of http://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=uriserv:OJ.L_.2014.25... )

Perhaps we should we introducing "transportable" as another principle.
e.g. You have moved from the dept. of finance to the dept. of communications (So your email and attributes have changed). No reason you shouldn't use the same credentials, or attributes, across departments of gov. i.e. access to some (an agreed) level of security and shared resources between departments. Once the trust is established of course.

We would need to start talking about "citizen-centric", because "user" is an unacceptable term in many countries that have already overcome the division of services, and authority, between central, (state) and local governments. Besides we have all sorts of a citizen's attributes being offered by different departments of 3 govs. One discussion seems to go missing in Aus. and UK. Departmental employees all serve the same citizen, and sometimes, as citizens, are served. i.e. levels of security

Longer term, talking to people as "citizens" also does away with all the other hats everyone wears. i.e. student, teacher (in the edu networks), private, public (in the collaboration space), employee,customer (between departments of govs). That's why ID is so powerful in bringing things together. Ideally, we all only want one (which helps personalize services), yet retains levels of security/privacy as one works between networks.

You've presented two opposing principles here. Collaborative vs. Competitive. So that's a basic confusion. Yes, "Highly reputable credential providers will compete to provide choice and convenience for users". And they could be either private, or public, institutions/departments. The collaboration will be in coming up with the standards and practices which are shared in common. Competition will, eventually, be driven by the trust citizens place in a single institution (or group of institutions) as their (primary) ID provider(s), and vote with their feet.

You'd appreciate that the UK gov has incentivized (credentialed) private companies by offering them a fee to act as ID providers. And excluded local councils from being involved. So they've created some confusion by their lack of competitiveness between the private and public sectors. Please stay across this conversation. https://identityassurance.blog.gov.uk/2015/06/30/gov-uk-verify-how-we-us...

One principle from Gary Sterrenberg. Pull not Push. http://www.themandarin.com.au/33039-mygov-juggernaut-tackles-customer-fa...

Lastly, you might like this doc. Philosophy precedes Principles. This doc captures a modern world citizen-centric approach. https://mydex.org/blog/2013/11/29/the-role-of-the-individual-in-digital-... As you know, the modern world talks about the "sharing" economy, while govs still talk about "delivering"; independently of course. Don't know if you'll ever get them to change that (old-fashioned) Principle.
